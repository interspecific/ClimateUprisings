In early 2024, legislators in Maryland, Massachusetts, New York, and Vermont introduced landmark Climate Superfund bills aimed at requiring major fossil fuel producers and refiners to pay for the staggering costs of climate adaptation. These bills would raise hundreds of billions of dollars for state-level infrastructure and resilience initiatives, with similar proposals expected in California and Minnesota.

These efforts are modeled after the federal Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act of 1980 (CERCLA), also known as Superfund. Like CERCLA, the Climate Superfund bills apply the “polluter pays principle,” holding fossil fuel corporations strictly liable for climate damages linked to greenhouse gas emissions—regardless of negligence or direct wrongdoing.

What makes these bills especially powerful is their reliance on emerging climate attribution science. This growing field—especially the subfield of source attribution—quantifies how much specific companies, countries, or sectors have contributed to global warming, sea level rise, and other climate harms. These insights provide a scientific basis for assigning financial responsibility to top emitters.

Naturally, the fossil fuel industry is pushing back hard. If passed, the bills are expected to face intense legal challenges. Yet precedent from traditional CERCLA cases shows that apportioning liability among multiple parties is possible—even if politically and administratively complex.

Regardless of their legal fate, these bills mark a historic shift: states are no longer waiting for federal action to hold polluters accountable. As climate disasters grow more severe, state-level leadership is setting the pace for climate justice, fiscal responsibility, and moral clarity.
