We Know What Works. The Problem Is Political, Not Scientific.

Don’t buy the narrative that climate solutions are vague or unproven. We have decades of data from climate science, geology, conservation, and planetary research. We know how land use affects carbon storage. We know how forests, wetlands, and soils absorb emissions and reduce floods, heat, and erosion.

Land, Life & Limits: A Planetary-Scale Plan to Heal the Climate

Natural climate solutions—such as reforestation, wetland restoration, and regenerative farming—can provide up to one-third of the carbon mitigation needed by 2030 to limit global warming to below 2°C (Griscom et al., 2017). In the United States alone, these actions could mitigate up to 1.2 billion metric tons CO₂e annually (Fargione et al., 2018).

  • Venus experienced runaway greenhouse warming; Mars lost its atmosphere—both are reminders of Earth's fragility (Way et al.).
  • Earth’s past climate shifts occurred over millennia. Today, CO₂ is rising at least 100× faster than natural cycles (IPCC AR6).
  • The Holocene’s stability allowed civilization to flourish. But now, the Anthropocene threatens to push Earth past tipping points.
  • Since early agriculture, humans have influenced atmospheric CO₂ levels (Ruddiman et al.).
  • Modern fossil fuel use and deforestation have dramatically altered the carbon balance, stressing ecosystems and weakening natural sinks (forests, oceans, soils).
  • These systems are approaching potential tipping points, risking feedback loops that would accelerate warming.

According to Fargione et al. (2018), the following interventions could deliver the majority of nature-based mitigation in the U.S. by 2025:

Reforestation / agroforestry50–60 million acres

  • Use peer-reviewed science: Ground planning in data from Griscom et al. (2017), Fargione et al. (2018), and IPCC SRCCL (2019).
  • Establish precise baselines: Map land cover, carbon stocks, and social vulnerability using national datasets (e.g., USDA NRI, NLCD, CDC SVI).
  • Map and prioritize zones: Overlay carbon potential with ecosystem services and equity to guide action—per Roe et al. (2019).
  • Set targets: Define time-bound acreage and CO₂-equivalent goals in alignment with 1.5°C pathways.
  • Integrate ecosystem modeling: Quantify co-benefits like water retention, heat resilience, and biodiversity support.
  • Develop robust monitoring: Use LiDAR, satellite imagery, field surveys, and citizen science for transparency.
  • Align with global frameworks: Coordinate with NDCs and LT-LEDS under the Paris Agreement.
  • Critical systems like Arctic sea ice, Greenland/Antarctica ice sheets, and the Amazon may soon reach points of no return.
  • Crossing these tipping points could trigger irreversible warming and biosphere destabilization (IPCC AR6).

This plan frames climate action not just as emissions reduction, but as a moral and ecological act of stewardship. Whether we reforest, restore wetlands, or green cities, we are rebalancing the carbon cycle and protecting planetary habitability.

Science confirms that nature-based solutions can deliver a significant portion of climate mitigation needed (Griscom et al.; Fargione et al.). But their power also lies in co-benefits: healthier soils, cleaner air, restored ecosystems, and stronger, more just communities.

  • Expand federal programs for reforestation and agroforestry (e.g., USDA Forest Service, NRCS EQIP).
  • Scale regenerative agriculture and soil carbon practices via incentives and insurance reform.
  • Develop national strategies for climate-resilient grazing lands.
  • Fund wetland, peatland, and riparian zone restoration through EPA and Army Corps of Engineers.
  • Establish prairie and grassland protections, especially in high-carbon ecoregions.
  • Launch urban canopy initiatives to combat heat islands and reduce inequality in tree cover.

Rebalancing Earth’s systems isn’t just science—it’s a sacred responsibility. It means defending the web of life, ensuring intergenerational justice, and changing our relationship to nature—from domination to reciprocity.
