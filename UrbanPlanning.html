Urban Planning & Land Use Reform

Building Equitable, Sustainable, and Climate-Resilient Communities

Urban land use in America has long been shaped by exclusionary policies that prioritized segregation, sprawl, and car dependency. This plan reverses that legacy by centering equity, affordability, and climate resilience in our cities' design.

  • Eliminate single-family-only zoning: Legalize duplexes, triplexes, and fourplexes everywhere.
  • Legalize accessory dwelling units (ADUs): Allow backyard cottages and basement apartments by right.
  • Abolish minimum parking requirements: Free up space for housing and reduce car dependency.
  • Reform zoning codes: Replace outdated setback, lot size, and height restrictions.
  • Adopt form-based codes: Prioritize walkability and design over land use separation.
  • Protect residents from displacement: Pair reforms with tenant protections and land trusts.
  • Incentivize transit-oriented development: Support density near transit hubs.
  • Repurpose underused land: Convert vacant lots and parking to community assets.
  • Inclusionary zoning for affordable housing
  • Density bonuses for green building and proximity to transit
  • Public acquisition of abandoned properties
  • Urban growth boundaries to limit sprawl
  • Fast-track green retrofits and adaptive reuse

Land use reform addresses the root causes of climate change, racial segregation, and housing inequality. Compact, mixed-use development lowers carbon emissions, fosters social integration, and reduces municipal costs. These strategies also enhance public health, support local economies, and build safer, more walkable communities.

This approach aligns with broader climate efforts like public transit expansion, environmental remediation, and equitable job creation—forming a cohesive blueprint for transformation.

Urban Greenspace and Urban Forestry

A Blueprint for Healthy, Resilient, and Equitable Cities

Urban greenspace and forestry are critical infrastructure, offering solutions for climate adaptation, health, environmental justice, and community resilience. This plan centers investments in historically marginalized neighborhoods to ensure that every resident has access to nature’s benefits.

  • Plant one billion urban trees: Prioritize underserved, heat-vulnerable areas.
  • Guarantee greenspace access: Ensure every resident lives within ¼ mile of a park.
  • Convert vacant land: Transform lots and malls into parks, gardens, and meadows.
  • Establish ecological corridors: Connect habitats to support biodiversity and resilience.
  • Green every schoolyard: Create outdoor learning spaces with native plants.
  • Use native, diverse species: Prioritize ecological resilience over monocultures.
  • Enact tree preservation laws: Protect mature trees with local ordinances.
  • Monitor with geospatial tools: Track canopy cover, access, and heat vulnerability.

Urban trees sequester carbon, lower temperatures, intercept stormwater, and improve air quality. But historically redlined communities have 23% less canopy, exacerbating health and climate disparities. Equitable urban greening is a public health, environmental, and racial justice imperative.

Urban forestry complements land use reform, green transportation, environmental remediation, and green jobs programs—building healthier, cooler, and more equitable cities.

Public Transportation Transformation

Building a Sustainable, Equitable Mobility System

The U.S. transportation system has long prioritized cars over people. This plan outlines a bold shift to a low-carbon, people-first system centered around equity, access, and environmental sustainability.

  • Expand urban transit: High-frequency BRT, light rail, and streetcars in dense corridors.
  • Retrofit streets: Build complete streets for pedestrians, cyclists, and buses.
  • Electrify transit fleets: All buses and trains zero-emission by 2035.
  • Eliminate/reduce fares: Treat transit as a public good to boost ridership and equity.
  • Integrate first/last-mile connections: Connect transit with bikes, scooters, and shuttles.
  • National high-speed rail: Federal investment in fast, frequent rail across metro regions.
  • Modernize Amtrak corridors: Electrification, reliability upgrades, and more frequent service.
  • Create intermodal hubs: Seamless transfers between rail, bus, bike, and walking routes.
  • Ban short-haul flights: Where rail under 2.5 hours exists, prioritize rail over air travel.
  • Invest in underserved areas: Prioritize communities harmed by past transportation policy.

Transportation is the U.S.'s largest source of GHG emissions. Public transit decarbonizes mobility, reduces air pollution, and makes cities healthier and more affordable. Car-free access increases equity and economic opportunity for all.

This transformation connects with broader goals—land use reform, job creation, remediation, and public health—to build sustainable and just communities.
